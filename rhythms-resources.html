Rhythms for Life Resources

The season of Lent is a time for us to slow down, fast and pray as we await Easter Sunday morning. Traditionally, Christians have observed the 40 days of Lent by practicing the spiritual disciplines as a way of enriching and strengthening our relationship with Christ. This Lent, we invite you to journey alongside us as we practice the disciplines together. Each week, we are providing practical ways for you to incorporate the disciplines into your life, no matter what the rhythm of your season. We’ll also provide additional resources for further study.

Week 1 ‐ Fasting

Reflective Question

How would you describe your current rhythm of life? Is it fast paced? Is it slow? Or is it more like jazz music, with fits and starts?

Description

Fasting is a voluntary abstinence from something for spiritual purposes. In the Bible, fasting is exclusively about abstaining from food, though there are many other ways to practice fasting. This practice helps us to tame our body’s impulses so that we hunger most for God. Donald Whitney says, “Fasting can be a testimony — even one directed to yourself — that you find your greatest pleasure and enjoyment in life from God. It’s a way of demonstrating to yourself that you love God more than food, that seeking Him is more important to you than eating, that Jesus — the Bread of heaven — is more satisfying to you than earthly bread.”

Practice

For the 40 days of Lent, we encourage you to fast from something. Maybe you adopt the ancient practice of abstaining from food on Wednesdays and Fridays until sundown. Maybe it’s a particular food or beverage that you choose to abstain from. Or maybe you need to fast from something else — social media, listening to music in the car, or video games. When you find yourself with the desire to engage in the activity that you are fasting from, use that as a time for prayer. In this way, fasting becomes feasting; as we fast from something, we feast on God’s goodness and holiness

Week 2 – Study

Reflective Question

How is your current practice of studying God’s Word? Do you only read it on the screens at church? Is it hard to find time in your current life rhythm to engage in the Scriptures? Or are you feasting on the Word of God daily, allowing it to shape your worldview, your habits and your interactions with others?

Description

The spiritual practice of study involves actively engaging with the Bible. Donald Whitney says, “No Spiritual Discipline is more important than the intake of God’s Word.” As Christians, we should strive to have the Word of God shape our lives more than any other resource available to us. Yet far too many of us rarely, if ever, read the Scriptures. The goal of this practice is not information, but formation. We do not study the Scriptures like we study for a math test; instead, we study it so that our lives can bear fruit.

Practice

Set aside 15 minutes each day this week to study God’s Word. Maybe that will require you to wake up earlier each morning, or stay up a bit later each evening. Find a place in your home that will help you avoid distractions. You may need to leave your phone and/or laptop in a different room. As you read, find one verse, word, concept or phrase that you will pledge to remember throughout the day. Don’t know where to start? We recommend the Gospel According to John or 1 John.

Week 3 ‐ Prayer

Reflective Question

What grade would you give your current prayer life? Is it a B-? An A? Or an F? Quick note here, I have never met a single person that would give themselves an A+! What are the practical steps you think you can take to get your grade up?

Description

Paul Miller says, “Prayer is simply the medium through which we experience and connect to God.” It’s a two-way conversation, a way that we commune with God. In prayer, we both talk and listen. We tell God our needs, we praise Him for His goodness, we thank Him for the blessings in our lives, and we ask for forgiveness for our sins. At the same time, we quietly listen for what the Holy Spirit is saying to us. Prayer does not have to be polished or pretty; through Jesus Christ, we can approach the throne of God in confidence, knowing that our simple words are enough.

Practice

First, set aside periods of time this week to pray. If you spent 15 minutes in Scripture last week, maybe this week you study the Bible for 10 and pray for 5. This might be very difficult at first. Your mind might wander, you might be distracted with the many tasks of the day, and you might find it hard to be still. Lean in to the uncomfortableness of it; we promise it will get easier!

Second, make a list of people to pray for each day. Think of family members, friends, fellow church members and world leaders. Pray for their healing and wholeness, and for them to see the favor of God in their lives.

Third, try praying through Scripture. The Psalms are a great place to start. If you find it difficult to know what to pray for, use the words of the psalmist to direct your prayers.

Finally, find natural ways to incorporate prayer into the rhythm of your life. Spend extended time in prayer before meals. Pray at stoplights instead of checking your phone. Before you go to the next meeting or next event, take some time to pray for that meeting or event.

Week 4 - The Act of Adoration

Reflective Question

When you pray, do you begin with more of a salutation than adoration? Do you quickly jump into making requests from God? How might it change your view of God to spend time in practicing adoration?

Description

Adoration is the spontaneous yearning of the heart to worship, honor and magnify God. When we practice adoration, it reminds us of the character and nature of God. We all need to be reminded of who God is because our understanding of God shapes our faith. When we stand in awe and wonder of the God who created us, we worry less about ourselves because our eyes are turned on God.

Practice

Each day, spend a few minutes starting with the practice of adoration. Using the resource provided, choose one or two attributes of God, praise God for His nature and character. Worship God for who He is. If time allows, continue the ACTS prayer model.

Week 5 ‐ Sabbath

Reflective Question

Does your current rhythm of life have space for true Sabbath? Why or why not?

Description

From the very beginning of Scripture, God commands that we take a Sabbath. Genesis 2 says that God created everything in the universe in six days and rested on the seventh. He did this not because He needed rest, but because He knew we would. God created a day off to be a part of the rhythm of our world, and when we neglect it, we only hurt ourselves. H. H. Farmer once said, “If you go against the grain of the universe, you get splinters.”

Practice

Take a day off! This week, do your best to devote 24 hours to things that bring you joy and satisfaction in God. Maybe that’s playing golf or working in your garden. Maybe that’s eating good food and sitting on the couch with your family. Whatever it is, find something restful and give your body and your brain a break.

This week, also try to focus on your sleep habits. Sleeping at night is a microrhythm of Sabbath that reminds us that even when we are not working, God still is. Strive for at least eight hours each night, and journal about how you feel each day.

Week 6 - The Art of Meditation

Reflective Question

How comfortable are you with silence and solitude? Do you recognize the ways you use "noise" as a cover-up? What would have to change in your life to spend time alone in silence with God, meditating and listening?

Description

There is a place in our faith for both deep, academic study of Scripture and slow, contemplative meditation of Scripture. When we meditate, we reflect on Scripture in a thoughtful, open way that invites God to fill our minds with the thoughts of God, renew our souls and transform us. You don’t have to be a monk or find a mountaintop to sit on. Just slow down. Don’t be in a hurry, and rest in the presence of God.

Practice

Scripture meditation is done best along with the practices of silence and solitude. This week, find a few minutes of time to be alone and quiet. Using the resources provided, meditate on these verses provided for Holy Week. Grap a pen and paper to take note of anything God might say to you during your meditation.
